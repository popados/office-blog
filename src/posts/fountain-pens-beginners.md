Most people try their first fountain pen the wrong way: they buy a cheap, scratchy pen from a discount bin, have a bad experience, and conclude that fountain pens "aren't for them." The truth is they just started with a bad pen.

You don't need to spend $150 to get a great fountain pen experience. You need to spend $25–$50 on the right one.

## What Makes a Good Beginner Fountain Pen

**Smooth nib out of the box.** Budget pens vary in quality control. The best beginner pens are consistent — you get a good writer, not a lottery ticket.

**Easy filling system.** Cartridge/converter pens are the easiest to start with. Cartridges snap in like printer ink. When you're ready, a converter lets you use bottled ink (better selection, much cheaper per ml).

**Available in extra-fine or fine.** Western fine nibs write broader than you'd expect. For note-taking and general writing, a fine or extra-fine is more practical than a medium.

## The 5 Picks

### 1. Lamy Safari — ~$30

The Safari is the default beginner recommendation for good reason. The nib is consistently smooth from unit to unit, the grip section is comfortable for most hand sizes, and it takes standard Lamy cartridges plus a converter (sold separately, ~$8).

The plastic body is more durable than it looks. It's survived being dropped, tossed in bags, and used daily for months without any issues.

**Nib sizes available:** EF, F, M, B, 1.1 stub  
**Recommended nib:** Fine (F)

### 2. Pilot Metropolitan — ~$20

The Metropolitan punches well above its price. The steel nib writes with surprising smoothness, and Pilot's quality control is excellent — you're very unlikely to get a bad one.

The Con-20 squeeze converter is included in most bundles, which means you can use bottled ink immediately. Pilot Iroshizuku inks work beautifully in this pen.

**Nib sizes available:** F, M  
**Recommended nib:** Fine (F)

### 3. TWSBI Eco — ~$33

The Eco is a demonstrator (clear body) with a piston fill mechanism — no cartridges needed. You fill directly from a bottle, and the large ink capacity means infrequent refills.

The piston fill is slightly more involved than cartridge pens but teaches you how fountain pen mechanisms work. Great step-up from a basic cartridge pen.

**Nib sizes available:** EF, F, M, B, 1.1 stub, 1.5 stub  
**Recommended nib:** Fine (F) or EF for small handwriting

### 4. Platinum Preppy — ~$5

At $5, the Preppy is practically a free trial. The nib is surprisingly smooth for the price, and Platinum's slip-and-seal cap keeps the ink from drying out for months — even if you leave the pen capped.

Don't let the price fool you into expecting a bad experience. The Preppy is a genuine fountain pen, not a toy.

**Best for:** Absolute beginners who want to try fountain pens before committing more

### 5. Kaweco Sport — ~$27

The Sport's compact size is its defining feature. Capped, it fits in a shirt pocket. Posted (cap on the back), it writes at a comfortable full length. The steel nib is consistent if not exceptional.

Great choice if you want a pocket pen that doesn't look like an office supply.

## Getting Started: The Short Version

1. Buy the **Lamy Safari** or **Pilot Metropolitan** — either is a reliable first pen
2. Use the included cartridge to start
3. When it runs dry, add a converter and try bottled ink (Pilot Iroshizuku, Diamine, or Waterman are all good entry points)
4. Write on decent paper — cheap paper causes feathering that makes any fountain pen look worse than it is

That's it. The rest — nib tuning, ink mixing, vintage pens — can wait until you're sure you like writing with one.
